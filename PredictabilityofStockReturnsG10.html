Heteroskedasticity occurs when the cariance of residuals changes with the level of predicted values. In this regression, this is not the case. The variance in residuals remains constant, even as the predicted values increase. As such, we can say that these residuals do not exhibit heteroskedasticity.

Autocorrelation is said to occur when the error terms of the dependent variable are not independent. Because the Durbin-Watson test produces a statistic close to 2, this OLS regression does not experience autocorrelation.

This comparsion shows that the HAC standard error is smaller than the OLS standard error, with 0.18 being smaller than 0.20. The data shows that the HAC standard errors are smaller in both cases of const and dynyLag12. This shows that the OLS standard errors could have been overestimated due to the fact that HAC standard errors adjust for heteroskedasticity and autocorrelation. OLS assumes homoscedasticity and no autocorrelation which defers from HAC which incorporates stronger estimates under more foreseen conditions which can explain the difference.
